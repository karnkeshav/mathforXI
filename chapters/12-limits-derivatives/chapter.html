How to Solve using Direct Substitution:

Step 1: Check if the function is defined at the approach point (no $0/0$).

Step 2: Substitute the value directly into the variable.

1. $5x+1$ as $x\to2$: Substitute $x=2 \implies 5(2)+1 = 11$.

2. $x^2$ as $x\to4$: Substitute $x=4 \implies 4^2 = 16$.

3. $3x^2$ as $x\to1$: Substitute $x=1 \implies 3(1)^2 = 3$.

4. $1/x$ as $x\to5$: Substitute $x=5 \implies 1/5$.

5. Continuity: Use this method for polynomials and simple rational functions where the denominator is non-zero.

Solving Indeterminate Forms ($0/0$):

Strategy A: Factoring. Break down polynomials to cancel the term causing $0/0$.

Strategy B: Rationalization. Used when square roots are present.

Strategy C: Power Formula. Apply $\lim \frac{x^n-a^n}{x-a}$ when powers are high.

1. $\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}$ at 2: Factor $x^2-4$ into $(x-2)(x+2)$. Cancel $(x-2)$. Remaining: $x+2$. Substitute $x=2 \implies 4$.

2. $\frac{\sqrt{1+x}-1}{x}$ at 0: Multiply top and bottom by $(\sqrt{1+x}+1)$. Numerator becomes $(1+x)-1 = x$. Cancel $x$. Substitute $x=0 \implies 1/(\sqrt{1}+1) = 1/2$.

3. $\frac{x^{10}-1}{x^5-1}$ at 1: Use the formula twice or divide top/bottom by $(x-1)$. Result: $\frac{10(1)^9}{5(1)^4} = 2$.

Standard trig limits form base of calculus
All Formulas (Trig Limits)
$$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}=1$$ $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\tan x}{x}=1$$ $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x}=0$$ $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin ax}{x}=a$$ $$\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x-1}{x}=1$$

Mastering Trig Transformations:

Rule: The angle in the function must match exactly with the denominator.

1. $\frac{\sin4x}{x}$: We need $4x$ in denominator. Multiply and divide by 4: $4 \times \frac{\sin4x}{4x}$. As $x \to 0$, $\frac{\sin4x}{4x} \to 1$. Result: $4(1) = 4$.

2. $\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$: Use trig identity $1-\cos x = 2\sin^2(x/2)$. Expression becomes $\frac{2\sin^2(x/2)}{x^2}$. Adjust denominator to $(x/2)^2$ by multiplying/dividing by 4. Result: $2 \times \frac{1}{4} = 1/2$.

3. $\frac{e^{\sin x}-1}{x}$: Write as $\frac{e^{\sin x}-1}{\sin x} \times \frac{\sin x}{x}$. Both parts approach 1. Result: $1 \times 1 = 1$.

Derivative gives rate of change
All Formulas (Derivatives)
$$\frac{d}{dx}(x^n)=nx^{n-1}$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(c)=0$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(u+v)=u'+v'$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(uv)=u'v+uv'$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{u}{v}\right)=\frac{vu'-uv'}{v^2}$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}(\sin x)=\cos x$$

Rules of Engagement:

1. Power Rule: Use for $x^n$. Step: Bring $n$ forward, subtract 1 from power.

2. Product Rule (uv): Use when two functions multiply. Step: (First $\times$ deriv of Second) + (Second $\times$ deriv of First).

3. Quotient Rule (u/v): Use for fractions. Step: (Bottom $\times$ deriv of Top - Top $\times$ deriv of Bottom) / (Bottom squared).

1. $x\sin x$: Let $u=x, v=\sin x$. $u'=1, v'=\cos x$. Result: $(x)(\cos x) + (\sin x)(1) = x\cos x + \sin x$.

2. $\frac{x}{x+1}$: $u=x, v=x+1$. $u'=1, v'=1$. Result: $\frac{(x+1)(1) - (x)(1)}{(x+1)^2} = \frac{1}{(x+1)^2}$.

First Principles

$[f(x+h)-f(x)]/h$
$h\to0$
$f(x)=x^2$
$f(x)=x^3$
Foundation of derivatives
All Formulas (First Principles)
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$ $f(x)=x^2\Rightarrow2x$
$f(x)=x^3\Rightarrow3x^2$

Step-by-Step Derivation for $f(x)=x^2$:

Step 1: Identify $f(x+h) = (x+h)^2 = x^2 + 2xh + h^2$.

Step 2: Find the difference $f(x+h) - f(x) = (x^2 + 2xh + h^2) - x^2 = 2xh + h^2$.

Step 3: Divide by $h \implies \frac{2xh+h^2}{h} = 2x + h$.

Step 4: Apply limit $h \to 0$. Result: $2x$.

Use this whenever a question asks: "Find derivative from definition" or "from first principles".
