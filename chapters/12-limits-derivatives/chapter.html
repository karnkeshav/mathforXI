Why Calculus Exists

Before calculus, mathematics was very good at answering questions like: “How much in total?” and “What is the average?”

But many real-world questions are different. They ask:

  • How fast is a car moving right now?
  • How quickly is a population growing at this moment?
  • What is the slope of a curve at one exact point?

Algebra cannot answer these questions. Calculus was invented to handle change that happens continuously.

Think: Why is “average speed” not the same as “speed at an instant”?

Approaching a Point

Imagine you are walking toward a door. With every step, you get closer.

Even before touching the door, you can clearly see where you are going.

Mathematics does something similar. Instead of jumping directly to a point, it looks at what happens as we move closer and closer.

Key idea: Getting close is different from actually reaching.

What Is a Limit?

A limit describes what a value is trying to become as we approach a point.

The important idea is:

  • The value at the point may not matter
  • The behaviour near the point is what matters

This is why limits are about behaviour, not substitution.

A limit answers: “What number do we get closer and closer to?”

What Is a Derivative?

A derivative measures how fast something changes at one exact instant.

For example:

  • Speed is the derivative of distance
  • Slope is the derivative of a curve

The derivative is built using limits. First we understand approaching, then limits, and finally derivatives.

Big picture: Derivative = limit of average change
