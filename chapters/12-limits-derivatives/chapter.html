Why Do We Study Limits and Derivatives?

Imagine you are travelling in a car. The speedometer needle is moving continuously.

At every instant, the speed is different. But algebra only knows how to find average speed.

Calculus was invented to answer:
“What is happening at this exact moment?”

You are not learning new formulas yet. You are learning a new way of thinking.

Approaching a Point

Suppose we want to know the value of a function near x = 2.

xf(x)
1.93.8
1.993.98
2.014.02
2.14.2

Even before x becomes exactly 2, we already see where the value is heading.

Checkpoint 1

If x keeps getting closer to 2, what is f(x) getting closer to?

Now We Introduce the Formula

All the ideas you just saw are written in short as:

lim (x → 2) f(x) = 4

The symbol is new, but the idea is already familiar.

Worked Example (NCERT Style)

Find the limit of f(x) = x² as x approaches 3.

xx²
2.98.41
2.998.9401
3.019.0601

Values are getting closer to 9.

Therefore, lim (x → 3) x² = 9
